Course 5: The Cognitive Science of Education

← Back to Courses

Course Description

This course delves into the interdisciplinary field of cognitive science, exploring how insights from psychology, neuroscience, linguistics, and computer science can inform and improve educational practices. It examines fundamental cognitive processes such as memory, attention, problem-solving, and reasoning, and their direct implications for designing effective learning environments and instructional strategies.

Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this course, students will be able to:

  • Explain core concepts and theories from cognitive science relevant to education.
  • Analyze how cognitive processes (e.g., memory, attention) influence learning outcomes.
  • Apply cognitive science principles to design evidence-based instructional strategies.
  • Critically evaluate educational interventions from a cognitive science perspective.
  • Understand the neural bases of learning and their implications for educational practice.

Weekly Syllabus

Week 1: Introduction to Cognitive Science and Education

Topics: What is cognitive science? Its interdisciplinary nature. Historical overview of cognitive science in education. Bridging the gap between research and practice. The cognitive revolution.

Learning Activities: Discussion on the relevance of cognitive science to everyday learning, analyzing common misconceptions about how the brain learns.

Week 2: Memory and Learning

Topics: Working memory vs. long-term memory. Encoding, storage, and retrieval processes. Strategies for effective memory (e.g., spaced practice, interleaving, retrieval practice). The testing effect.

Learning Activities: Applying memory strategies to personal learning, designing activities that promote long-term retention.

Week 3: Attention and Engagement

Topics: Types of attention. Factors influencing attention in the classroom. Cognitive load theory. Strategies for managing cognitive load and enhancing engagement. Multitasking and learning.

Learning Activities: Analyzing classroom scenarios for attention challenges, proposing strategies to optimize attention and reduce cognitive load.

Week 4: Problem-Solving and Reasoning

Topics: Models of problem-solving. Heuristics and biases. Developing critical thinking skills. Inductive and deductive reasoning. Transfer of learning. The role of expertise.

Learning Activities: Solving educational problems using cognitive strategies, analyzing examples of expert vs. novice problem-solving.

Week 5: Metacognition and Self-Regulated Learning

Topics: What is metacognition? Planning, monitoring, and evaluating learning. Strategies for fostering self-regulated learning. The importance of reflection. Growth mindset.

Learning Activities: Developing metacognitive strategies, designing interventions to promote self-regulated learning in students.

Week 6: The Neuroscience of Learning

Topics: Basic brain structure and function relevant to learning. Neurons and synapses. Brain plasticity. Impact of sleep, stress, and emotion on learning. Neuro-myths in education.

Learning Activities: Discussing the implications of neuroscience for educational practice, debunking common brain-based learning myths.

Week 7: Cognitive Science of Reading and Mathematics

Topics: Cognitive processes involved in reading (e.g., phonological awareness, decoding, comprehension). Cognitive foundations of mathematical understanding. Effective instructional approaches for literacy and numeracy based on cognitive science.

Learning Activities: Analyzing reading and math difficulties from a cognitive perspective, evaluating evidence-based interventions.

Week 8: Designing Cognitively Informed Instruction

Topics: Synthesizing course knowledge. Principles of instructional design based on cognitive science. Creating effective learning materials. The role of feedback. Capstone project: designing a cognitively informed lesson or intervention.

Learning Activities: Developing a lesson plan incorporating cognitive science principles, peer review and presentations.
